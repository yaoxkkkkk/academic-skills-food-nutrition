# Reasoning & Logical Fallacies

Used by `synthesizer` (build sound argument) and `critic` (attack it). Also useful
to `food-review`'s devil's advocate.

## Sound synthesis
- **Claim → evidence → warrant:** every claim names its evidence and why that
  evidence supports it. Keep the claim proportionate to the evidence.
- **Weight by quality:** a confirmed RCT/systematic review outranks a single
  in-vitro or single-batch study (see `source-quality-hierarchy.md`).
- **Show disagreement:** present conflicts and explain their cause (matrix,
  method, dose, population); do not average them into false consensus.
- **State confidence** and its basis (quantity, consistency, quality, directness).

## Fallacies to catch (common in food/nutrition claims)
- **Correlation ⇒ causation** — observational association read as a causal effect.
- **Extrapolation beyond the tested range** — in-vitro/animal dose → human diet;
  one matrix/cultivar → all foods; supraphysiological dose → dietary relevance.
- **Cherry-picking** — citing only supporting studies; ignoring null/negative results.
- **Pseudo-replication** — analytical replicates counted as biological n.
- **Surrogate-outcome overreach** — a biomarker change claimed as a health benefit.
- **Appeal to novelty / "natural"** — new or natural asserted as better without evidence.
- **HARKing** — hypothesizing after results are known, presented as a priori.
- **Publication/funding bias ignored** — industry-funded findings taken at face value.
- **Ecological fallacy** — population-level association applied to individuals.
- **Absence of evidence ⇒ evidence of absence** — "no significant effect" read as "no effect" (check power).

## Critic's job
For each major conclusion: what would make it wrong, is that ruled out by the
validated evidence, and which fallacy (if any) is it committing? Every objection
must be specific and actionable.
